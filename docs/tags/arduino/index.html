Turning a Dumb AC Unit Smart (Without Losing my Security Deposit)

2025-09-28 - 11 minute read
#c++  #arduino  #homeassistant  #esp32 

TL;DR: You can do some really useful, albeit janky stuff with a servo motor, an esp32, and a dream.

My rental apartment’s AC unit can only be controlled using these retro analog knob-based controls, mounted right onto the unit. They work, but having to stand up and and fiddle with them all the time got annoying pretty quickly.

Fortunately, there’s an ‘ol Prilik family saying that goes something like this: “there’s no problem in life that a servo motor and an esp32 can’t fix”1

And sure enough, after dropping ~$15 on parts, waiting a couple weeks for things to arrive from China, and spending a few hours iterating alongside my good friend Claude - I managed to hack together this beautiful mess:

What you’re looking at here is a beautifully jerry-rigged esp32-controlled servo motor, coupled to one of my AC unit’s knobs using a shaft coupler, all affixed to the AC’s back-plane using a cheap L-bracket and a binder clip (and a bit of industrial-grade cardboard padding).

This whole MacGyver’d up contraption then talks to my personal Home Assistant instance over MQTT, which turns the AC unit on/off based on the state of a temperature sensor located in the same the room.

Et voila! No longer must I get off the couch to tweak the AC anymore!

Very magical 🪄

For all you professional embedded and mechatronics folks out there: I strongly suggest you stop reading here.

The rest of this blog post is a walkthrough of a software engineer’s approach to home automation and building custom hardware, and let me tell you: both the final product, and the journey to get there, are hella jank.

That said… if you’re not afraid of a bit of jank: read onwardsm and join me on this fun foray into how I managed to hack together some totally bespoke home-automation hardware with almost no experience (or budget)!
